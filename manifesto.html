On Syria as a Continuous Geographic and Civilisational Nation

I. What Syria Means By “Syria,” I do not mean only the present-day Syrian Arab Republic on the map. I mean the wider Levant: the eastern Mediterranean lands that have long formed one historical and civilisational field, including Syria, Lebanon, Palestine, Jordan, and adjoining regions within the republics of Turkey and Egypt. The names changed across empires and languages; Syria, Phoenicia, Bilad al-Sham, the Levant. However the underlying region remained recognisable.

II. The Long Continuity of the Land From antiquity onward, major writers and geographers treated this space as one. Herodotus, Strabo, Josephus, Pliny, al-Muqaddasi, and Yaqut all describe a Syria that includes or surrounds the territories now divided into separate states.

III. The Modern Syrian Idea In the nineteenth century, this older frame reappeared in modern political language through the Nahda and Mahjar intellectual movements.

IV. Why Division Matters Fragmentation has weakened institutions and reduced a historically connected region into competing political units.

V. What This Movement Demands This text argues for a wider Levantine framework grounded in historical continuity across geography, language, and memory.
